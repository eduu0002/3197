Global Energy Crisis

The world is still battling the climate crisis with 87% of the world's greenhouse gases being a product of our energy production proccesses. However, the solution is not just simply to reduce use of fossil fuels. Below we will look at another energy crisis that is not often talked about.
Global Energy Production
In the last 10 years, both United States of America and China have been the largest producers of electricity by a large margin as compared to the rest of the world with North America being one of the largest importers of energy and Europe the largest exporters of energy.
Global Energy Consumption
The energy consumption per capita has decreased globally and we can see a steady increase in the production of cleaner fuels with countries like Canada and Brazil which has more than 80% of their electricity being produced from clean fuels. Countries like China and USA who are the largest consumers of fossil fuels have also increased their renewable energy production by 50% from 2000 - 2020.
Percentage Access to Energy vs GDP Per Capita
Majority of the world has made some slow and steady strides to renewable and clean energies however, there is another problem that needs to be addressed which can be seen on the left sides of these scatter plots. Countries with low GDP Per Capita less than 10,000 like Uganda and Ethiopia are struggling to have access to any energy at all. We can see from the first scatter plot renewable energy is a privilege only for wealthy countries. Poorer countries like Uganda and Ethiopia lack access to modern energy sources and, as a result, the majority of the population would have to rely on solid fuel sources like firewood. The use of firewood is predominantly used in homes which carries a high risk of respitory problems as combustion of firewood releases methane and carbon monoxide into the air.

While the world is struggling with global CO2 pollution, less developed countries are also struggling with indoor pollution due to energy poverty. Until these issues are addressed it seems unlikely that the world will be able to solve our current energy crisis anytime soon.
